## What to Look for Before Buying the Best Combination Square

### Find Your Need - Compatibility and use case considerations
The first step is determining how you will use the tool. If you are doing rough framing or outdoor construction, a 16-inch model with a high-visibility level is ideal. For fine woodworking or cabinet making, a 12-inch or 6-inch model with etched markings and a hardened steel blade is better. Consider if you need trade-specific features, like the magnetic adjustments found on electrician-focused models.

### Budget - Setting realistic price expectations ($15-$150 ranges)

## What Is the Best Way to Choose combination square?

The best way to choose a combination square is to test the sliding action and the locking mechanism. A good square should have a blade that slides smoothly without catching and locks firmly with very little effort. If the blade wiggles after being locked, it is not worth your money.

Follow this step-by-step guide for selection:
1. **Check for Light:** Hold the square against a known flat surface and look for light gaps between the blade and the workpiece.
2. **Perform the Flip Test:** Draw a line perpendicular to a straight edge, then flip the square over and see if the blade aligns with the same line. If it doesn't, the tool is not square.
3. **Examine the Markings:** Ensure the graduations are deeply etched and easy for you to read in your specific workspace lighting.
4. **Feel the Weight:** A heavier square often provides better stability when marking, but if you carry it all day, you might prefer a lightweight aluminum model.
5. **Consider the Set:** Buying a 6-inch and 12-inch set together is often more economical and covers almost all possible scenarios in a shop.

Avoid buying squares with plastic heads or very thin blades, as these will flex during use and lead to inaccurate measurements. If you are on a budget, prioritize a square with a stainless steel blade over an aluminum one for better longevity.

**What is the best combination square for beginners?**
For beginners, the Arrow 12-Inch or IRWIN 6-Inch are excellent choices. They are affordable, durable, and offer the core features needed for most home projects. They provide a great balance of accuracy and value without the high cost of professional machining tools.

**How much should I spend on combination square?**
Most DIYers will be happy in the $20 to $40 range. However, if you are doing serious woodworking where precision is paramount, spending $80 to $120 for a Starrett or iGaging set is a worthwhile investment that pays off in accuracy.

**What features are most important in combination square?**
The most important features are etched markings for durability, a stainless or hardened steel blade for rigidity, and a high-quality locking bolt that prevents the blade from shifting during use. A built-in bubble level is also very helpful.

**How do I maintain my combination square?**
Keep the blade clean and free of sawdust or glue. Periodically apply a light coat of machine oil to steel components to prevent rust. Ensure the groove in the ruler is clear of debris so the sliding action remains smooth.

**Are expensive combination square worth it?**
Yes, if you need professional-grade accuracy. Higher-priced squares are machined to tighter tolerances, meaning they are guaranteed to be 90 degrees out of the box. They also use better materials that won't wear down over years of use.

**What brands make the best combination square?**
Starrett is the industry leader for precision. Empire is excellent for trade work, especially for leveling. IRWIN and Arrow offer the best value for general use, while Klein Tools is the top choice for specialized electrical applications.

**How long do combination square typically last?**
A well-maintained steel and cast iron square can literally last a lifetime and be passed down through generations. Cheaper zinc or aluminum models may last 5 to 10 years of regular use before the locking mechanism or scales wear out.

**Can I find good combination square on a budget?**
Absolutely. Brands like WORKPRO and IRWIN offer metal-bodied squares that are surprisingly accurate for the price. The key is to check them for squareness yourself and keep them clean to extend their functional life.
