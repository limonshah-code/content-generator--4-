## What to Look for Before Buying the Best Nails For Shiplap

### 1. Find Your Need - Compatibility and use case considerations
Before you start, determine the type of wood you are using. If you have thin 1/4 inch reclaimed planks, you will need shorter 18-gauge brad nails or pin nails. However, for 3/4 inch solid wood shiplap, you will require 2-inch or 2.5-inch finishing nails to penetrate through the tongue or groove and into the wall stud. Consider if you want the nails to be hidden (blind nailing) or visible for a rustic look.

### 2. Budget - Setting realistic price expectations ($X-$Y ranges)
Hardware is generally inexpensive compared to the wood itself. A basic assortment kit ranges from $10 to $20 and is perfect for most DIY tasks. Specialized nails, like high-carbon steel for hardwoods or decorative rose gold options, may cost $25 to $40 per pound. Always buy about 10% more than you think you need to account for bent nails or mistakes.

### 3. Key Features - Most important technical specifications
Look for nails with a thin shank to prevent splitting, especially near the ends of the boards. High-carbon steel is preferred for driving into studs without bending. For shiplap, a small head (finish or pin nail) is essential so it can be easily countersunk and covered with wood filler if desired.

### 4. Quality & Durability - What to look for in construction
Ensure the nails are straight and have sharp tips. Galvanized or polished finishes are great for interior use to prevent rusting from any humidity in the air. If you are installing shiplap in a bathroom, look for stainless steel or specifically coated nails that can handle moisture without corroding or staining the wood.

### 5. Brand Reputation - Importance of established manufacturers
Brands like Mr. Pen, Hillman, and Maze Nails have long histories of producing reliable fasteners. Established brands often follow ASTM standards, ensuring that every nail in the box has the same strength and dimensions, which prevents your nail gun from jamming or your hammer from slipping.

### 6. Portability/Size - Considerations for space and storage
If you are doing a large project, an assortment kit in a divided box is much easier to manage than loose bags of nails. This keeps your workspace organized and prevents you from accidentally using the wrong size nail, which could result in a loose board or a split plank.

## Is it A Wise Decision to Buy nails for shiplap?

Investing in the right nails for shiplap is absolutely a wise decision for any homeowner or DIY enthusiast. While it might be tempting to use whatever leftover nails you have in the garage, shiplap requires specific fasteners to handle the natural expansion and contraction of wood. Standard common nails often have heads that are too large, which can ruin the smooth aesthetic of your wall, or shanks that are too thick, leading to split planks.

By choosing high-quality finishing or brad nails, you ensure that your investment in the wood planks is protected. Proper nails provide the necessary holding power to keep boards from warping off the wall over time, especially in environments with fluctuating humidity. Compared to adhesives alone, nails offer a mechanical bond that is much more reliable and easier to remove if you ever decide to change your decor. In the long run, the small extra cost of specialized nails saves you money by preventing damaged materials and the need for future repairs.

## What Is the Best Way to Choose nails for shiplap?

### Step-by-Step Selection Guide
1. **Measure Plank Thickness:** Your nail should be at least twice as long as the thickness of the plank plus the drywall, typically 1.5 to 2 inches for standard installs.
2. **Identify Wood Type:** Use high-carbon steel for hardwoods (Oak/Walnut) and standard steel or brad nails for softwoods (Pine/MDF).
3. **Select Gauge:** 18-gauge is the standard for most thin shiplap. 16-gauge is better for thicker, heavier boards.
4. **Choose Finish:** Match the nail color to the wood if you want them visible, or choose silver/zinc if you plan to paint over them.

### Professional Tips
- Always test a nail on a scrap piece of your shiplap first to check for splitting.
- Use a nail set tool to countersink nail heads below the surface for a professional look.
- Aim for the wall studs. Nails driven only into drywall will eventually pull out.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Using Nails That Are Too Short:** If the nail doesn't reach the stud, the shiplap will eventually sag.
- **Over-driving Nails:** If you drive the nail too deep, you might crack the tongue of the shiplap board.
- **Ignoring Grain Direction:** Driving a thick nail too close to the end of a board will almost always cause a split.
